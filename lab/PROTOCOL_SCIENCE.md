## 1. Carbohydrate Oxidation Science

### 1.1 Intestinal Transporter Physiology

Exogenous carbohydrate oxidation during exercise is constrained not by muscle metabolism but by intestinal absorption capacity. Two distinct transporter systems govern uptake across the enterocyte brush border:

**SGLT1 (Sodium-Glucose Linked Transporter 1):** The primary transporter for glucose and glucose-containing polymers (maltodextrin, maltose). Co-transports one glucose molecule with two sodium ions. Saturates at approximately 60g/hr of glucose equivalent under exercise conditions. This ceiling is not meaningfully expandable by consuming more glucose — excess substrate accumulates in the intestinal lumen, draws water osmotically, and produces diarrhea. The 60g/hr ceiling is physiological, not motivational.

**GLUT5 (Glucose Transporter 5):** A facilitated diffusion transporter with specificity for fructose. Operates independently of SGLT1 — uses a separate carrier protein and a separate apical membrane pathway. Crucially, GLUT5 does not compete with SGLT1 for capacity. Fructose absorption via GLUT5 runs in parallel with glucose absorption via SGLT1.

This dual-pathway architecture is the mechanistic foundation of Multiple Transportable Carbohydrates (MTCs).

*Sources: Jentjens & Jeukendrup (2003), Mueckler & Thorens (2013) [GLUT family review], Wright et al. (2011) [SGLT biology]*

### 1.2 Multiple Transportable Carbohydrates (MTCs)

The seminal finding of the Jeukendrup laboratory (University of Birmingham): combining glucose or maltodextrin with fructose at a 2:1 ratio by mass enables total exogenous carbohydrate oxidation of 1.26–1.75g/min, compared to ~1.0g/min maximum for glucose alone. This represents a 26–75% increase in utilisation efficiency.

**The benchmark numbers:**
- Glucose alone: maximum oxidation ~1.0g/min (~60g/hr)
- Glucose + fructose (2:1): oxidation up to 1.26g/min in early studies; up to 1.75g/min in trained athletes with gut adaptation
- At 90g/hr intake (2:1 MTC): oxidation ~90% efficient (very little appears in colon)
- At 120g/hr intake (2:1 MTC): oxidation remains high; this is the current physiological ceiling for most athletes

**Practical ceiling with MTCs: 90–120g/hr.** Above 120g/hr, intestinal transit capacity becomes the limiting factor regardless of transporter combination.

*Sources: Jeukendrup AE & Jentjens RLPG (2000). Oxidation of carbohydrate feedings during prolonged exercise. Sports Medicine; Jentjens RL et al. (2004). Oxidation of combined ingestion of glucose and fructose during exercise. J Appl Physiol; Jeukendrup AE (2010). Carbohydrate and exercise performance: the role of multiple transportable carbohydrates. Curr Sports Med Rep.*

### 1.3 The Jeukendrup Research Lineage

The mechanistic case for MTCs was established through a sequence of controlled trials across 2000–2010:

**Jeukendrup & Jentjens (2000):** Systematic review establishing that exogenous glucose oxidation is capped at ~1g/min during exercise. Identified SGLT1 saturation as the rate-limiting mechanism. This is the foundational paper for the 60g/hr glucose ceiling.

**Jentjens et al. (2004):** First direct demonstration that glucose + fructose (2:1) oxidation exceeds glucose alone — peak oxidation 1.26g/min vs 1.01g/min for glucose. Performance sustained longer at higher workloads. *J Appl Physiol 96(4):1278–1284.*

**Jeukendrup AE (2010):** Comprehensive review synthesising the MTC evidence base and formally establishing 90g/hr as the evidence-based ceiling with 2:1 glucose:fructose. Introduced the concept of "intestinal training" as a performance variable. *Curr Sports Med Rep 9(4):242–246.*

The 2010 paper is the primary citation for the 90g/hr recommendation across all major sports nutrition position stands published from 2010 onward.

### 1.4 Gut Adaptation: Cox et al. (2010)

**Citation:** Cox GR, Clark SA, Cox AJ, et al. Daily training with high carbohydrate availability increases exogenous carbohydrate oxidation during endurance cycling. *J Appl Physiol.* 2010;109(5):1520–1528.

**Design:** 28-day intervention. Trained cyclists completed daily sessions consuming 60–80g/hr of carbohydrate during exercise. Outcome measures: exogenous carbohydrate oxidation rate, gastrointestinal symptom frequency, and transporter protein expression (intestinal biopsy subset).

**Key findings:**
1. Exogenous carbohydrate oxidation increased by 17% (from ~0.83g/min to ~0.97g/min) after 28 days
2. Gastrointestinal symptom frequency (nausea, bloating, urgency) decreased significantly
3. SGLT1 protein expression increased in the trained group — direct transporter upregulation confirmed at the molecular level

**Mechanistic interpretation:** The intestinal epithelium responds to carbohydrate flux exactly as skeletal muscle responds to mechanical load — with adaptive upregulation of the relevant transport machinery. SGLT1 density increases with chronic high-carbohydrate exercise. GLUT5 expression likely increases in parallel (not measured in Cox 2010 but supported by subsequent in vitro work).

**Clinical implication:** An athlete who has never consumed 80g/hr in training cannot safely execute 80g/hr on race day. The gut must be trained. Gut training requires a minimum of 28 days; full adaptation (to reach the elite 90–120g/hr ceiling) requires 3–6 months of progressive exposure.

### 1.5 Carbohydrate Periodisation: Stellingwerff & Cox (2014)

**Citation:** Stellingwerff T, Cox GR. Systematic review: Carbohydrate supplementation on exercise performance or capacity of varying durations. *Appl Physiol Nutr Metab.* 2014;39(9):998–1011.

This systematic review distinguished between two distinct contexts of carbohydrate periodisation that are frequently conflated:

1. **"Train low" for mitochondrial adaptation:** Deliberately training in glycogen-depleted states (fasted training, sleep-low protocols) to upregulate fat oxidation and mitochondrial biogenesis markers (PGC-1α, AMPK). This is legitimate as an adaptation tool in the base phase. The review confirmed metabolic adaptations occur.

2. **"Race high" as the invariant rule:** Regardless of any training-phase periodisation, race-day carbohydrate availability must be maximised. No evidence supports reduced carbohydrate intake on race day to enhance performance. The "train low, race high" principle is explicitly **about fat adaptation, not about carbohydrate oxidation capacity** — an athlete who trains low does not thereby improve their ability to oxidise exogenous carbohydrate on race day. Gut adaptation requires glucose flux, not its absence.

**The common misinterpretation to avoid:** Some athletes interpret "train low" as license to underfuel on race day, believing fat-adapted metabolism will carry them. Stellingwerff & Cox (2014) does not support this. Fat oxidation max rate is ~1g/min (~60 kcal/min). At race pace (5+ kcal/min), the carbohydrate gap cannot be closed by fat. Any athlete attempting 90-120g/hr race protocols must gut-train at those levels in training — "train low" sessions are occasional adaptations, not the primary nutrition pattern.

### 1.6 Session-Length Dependency for Carbohydrate Intake

The evidence for intra-session carbohydrate intake is dose-dependent on exercise duration:

| Duration | Evidence-based recommendation | Mechanism |
|----------|------------------------------|-----------|
| <45 min | No intra-session carbs required | Endogenous glycogen sufficient; GI load without benefit |
| 45–75 min | Mouth rinse with CHO solution only | Oral carbohydrate receptors signal CNS → reduced RPE without GI burden (Carter et al. 2004) |
| 75 min – 2.5 hr | 30–60g/hr, single-source glucose acceptable | SGLT1 not yet saturated; fructose co-ingestion beneficial but not required |
| >2.5 hr | 60–90g/hr, MTC protocol required | SGLT1 saturation reached; fructose pathway needed to exceed 60g/hr |
| >4 hr (IM bike + run) | Full MTC protocol 60–120g/hr by bracket | Maximum substrate delivery required; gut adaptation now a key variable |

*Sources: Carter JM et al. (2004). The effect of carbohydrate mouth rinse on 1-h cycle time trial performance. Med Sci Sports Exerc; Burke LM et al. (2011). Carbohydrates for training and competition. J Sports Sci.*

## 2. Sodium, Fluid Balance and Electrolytes

### 2.1 Exercise-Associated Hyponatremia (EAH)

Hyponatremia is defined as serum sodium concentration below 135 mmol/L. Exercise-associated hyponatremia (EAH) is symptomatic below approximately 125 mmol/L (confusion, seizure, pulmonary oedema). EAH is a potentially fatal condition; it causes more race-related deaths in endurance sports than dehydration.

**Primary mechanism: dilutional, not deficiency-based.**

The overwhelming body of evidence — including the landmark work of Noakes and colleagues — establishes that EAH in endurance athletes is caused principally by overdrinking of hypotonic or plain water, not by sodium deficiency per se. An athlete who matches fluid intake to sweat rate cannot develop dilutional hyponatremia regardless of sodium intake, because net fluid volume remains stable. The athlete who drinks far in excess of sweat rate dilutes plasma sodium even if some electrolytes are consumed.

*Primary citation: Noakes TD. Waterlogged: The serious problem of overhydration in endurance sports. Human Kinetics, 2012.*
*Epidemiological foundation: Speedy DB, Noakes TD, Rogers IR, et al. Hyponatremia in ultradistance triathletes. Med Sci Sports Exerc. 1999;31(6):809–815.*

**EAH epidemiology in triathlon:**
- Speedy et al. (1999): screened 330 athletes at a New Zealand ultradistance triathlon; 18% were hyponatremic post-race; 4% symptomatic
- Strong predictor: weight gain during the race (i.e., net fluid overload, not deficit)
- Body weight loss of 1–4% during racing is safe, expected, and does not impair performance. Weight gain during racing = overdrinking

### 2.2 Dehydration Thresholds

Body weight loss thresholds and their performance/medical implications:

| Body weight loss (%) | Status |
|---------------------|--------|
| 1–2% | Mild dehydration; minimal performance impact at low intensity |
| 2–4% | Moderate dehydration; aerobic performance begins to decline (~5% reduction in VO₂max) |
| >4% | Significant performance impairment; increased cardiovascular strain |
| >8% | Medical emergency; circulatory compromise |

*Source: Sawka MN et al. (2007). American College of Sports Medicine Position Stand: Exercise and Fluid Replacement. Med Sci Sports Exerc.*

**Important nuance:** The 2% threshold is derived from heat stress studies. In cool conditions, athletes can sustain 3–4% deficit without meaningful performance loss. The threshold is not an absolute — it interacts with environmental conditions, exercise intensity, and individual acclimatisation status.

### 2.3 Sweat Sodium Concentration: Individual Variability

Sweat sodium concentration is among the most variable physiological parameters measured in sports science. Population range: 20–80 mmol/L (approximately 460–1,840 mg/L). This 4-fold range makes population-level sodium recommendations imprecise.

**Clinically relevant categories:**
- Low sodium sweat: <40 mmol/L (~920 mg/L) — approximately 20% of athletes
- Average: 40–60 mmol/L (~920–1,380 mg/L)
- High: >60 mmol/L (~1,380+ mg/L) — approximately 20% of athletes
- "Salty sweaters" (>80 mmol/L): white salt crust on skin/kit after drying; burning eyes from sweat; visible salt deposits on dark clothing

In the absence of sweat testing, population-average values should be used with conservative upward adjustment for athletes with salty sweat phenotypic markers.

*Source: Maughan RJ, Shirreffs SM. Development of individual hydration strategies for athletes. Int J Sport Nutr Exerc Metab. 2010;20(2):158–162.*

### 2.4 Sodium Targets by Environmental Condition

| Condition | Sodium target (mg/hr) | Rationale |
|-----------|----------------------|-----------|
| Temperate (<22°C, moderate humidity) | 500–1,000 | Conservative default; covers average sweat rate + sodium concentration |
| Hot (22–32°C or high humidity) | 1,000–1,200 | Sweat rate increases; sodium losses scale accordingly |
| Extreme heat (>32°C) | 1,200–1,500 | Upper range of sweat rates; additional sodium required to maintain plasma osmolality |

These targets are not ceilings — they are starting points. Athletes with confirmed salty sweat phenotype should target the upper bound of each range regardless of temperature.

*Source: McCubbin AJ, Costa RJS, et al. Sports Dietitians Australia Position Statement: Nutrition for Exercise in Hot Environments. Int J Sport Nutr Exerc Metab. 2019;29(4):351–402.*

### 2.5 Electrolyte Absorption Kinetics: Buccal vs GI

**Buccal absorption (sublingual/buccal mucosa):** Ionic species diffuse directly into capillary beds beneath the oral mucosa, bypassing the gastrointestinal tract entirely. Onset of systemic appearance: 2–5 minutes. This route is not subject to reduced blood flow during exercise.

**GI absorption:** Requires transport across enterocyte membranes, entry into hepatic portal circulation, first-pass hepatic processing. Onset: 30–60 minutes from ingestion to peak plasma appearance. Importantly, during high-intensity exercise, splanchnic blood flow is reduced by 40–80% compared to rest — this dramatically slows GI absorption of all substrates.

**Clinical implication for SaltStick FastChews:** Products that dissolve in the buccal cavity (FastChews format) deliver electrolytes into systemic circulation faster than swallowed capsules or tablets, and are not subject to the gut blood flow reduction that occurs during intense exercise. This makes buccal-delivery electrolyte products particularly appropriate for high-intensity portions of racing (late bike, run) when GI absorption is most compromised.

*Sources: ACSM Position Stand (Sawka et al. 2007); general pharmacokinetics of buccal absorption — Rathbone MJ, Drummond BK, Tucker IG. The oral cavity as a site for systemic drug delivery. Adv Drug Deliv Rev. 1994.*

## 3. Caffeine

### 3.1 ISSN Position Stand (2021)

**Citation:** Guest NS, VanDusseldorp TA, Nelson MT, et al. International Society of Sports Nutrition Position Stand: Caffeine and Exercise Performance. *J Int Soc Sports Nutr.* 2021;18(1):1.

**Headline recommendations:**
- Effective dose range: **3–6 mg/kg body weight**
- Timing of ingestion: **45–60 minutes prior to exercise** for maximum adenosine receptor occupancy at event start
- Evidence quality for aerobic endurance: Grade A (highest evidence level — multiple consistent RCTs)
- Effect size: 2–4% mean improvement in time-to-exhaustion and time-trial performance

### 3.2 Mechanism of Action

**Primary mechanism: Adenosine receptor antagonism.** Adenosine is a neuromodulator that accumulates during exercise and promotes fatigue sensations by binding A1 and A2A receptors in the CNS. Caffeine is a competitive antagonist at these receptors — structurally similar enough to occupy the binding site but does not activate the receptor. Net effect: reduced perception of effort (RPE) at equivalent workloads. The athlete works just as hard but perceives it as easier.

**Secondary mechanisms:**
- Stimulation of catecholamine release (adrenaline/epinephrine) → enhanced fat mobilisation
- Direct effects on skeletal muscle calcium release kinetics (at high doses)
- Possible glycogen-sparing effect via increased fat utilisation (mechanistically plausible; data inconsistent)

The RPE reduction mechanism is dominant and well-established. Glycogen-sparing is contested.

*Sources: Fredholm BB et al. (1999). Actions of caffeine in the brain. Pharmacol Rev; Spriet LL (2014). Exercise and sport performance with low doses of caffeine. Sports Med.*

### 3.3 GI Risk Profile of Caffeine

Caffeine stimulates gastric acid secretion via vagal nerve activation and increases lower oesophageal sphincter pressure (paradoxically, this can increase reflux). More importantly for endurance racing, caffeine increases gut motility — it is a recognised prokinetic agent. Combined with the mechanical impact forces of running, caffeine-induced motility increases significantly amplify the risk of urgency, diarrhea, and cramping during the run leg.

**Practical implication:** Caffeinated products taken during the run carry higher GI risk than equivalent products taken during the bike. The horizontal cycling position, lower mechanical impact, and better splanchnic blood flow on the bike make caffeine better tolerated there. Any athlete with GI history should treat caffeinated run-leg products as a risk modifier.

*Source: Nehlig A, Daval JL, Debry G. Caffeine and the central nervous system: mechanisms of action, biochemical, metabolic and psychostimulant effects. Brain Res Brain Res Rev. 1992.*

### 3.4 Distribution Strategy in Long Events

**Pharmacokinetics relevant to Ironman:**
- Time to peak plasma concentration: 45–60 minutes post-ingestion
- Half-life: 3–5 hours (interindividual range: 1.5–9.5 hours due to CYP1A2 genetic variation)
- Duration of measurable CNS effect: approximately 3–4 hours at peak

For a Full Ironman lasting 9–14 hours, a single pre-race dose of 300mg provides meaningful effect for approximately the swim and early bike. By mid-bike (~hour 4–5), plasma caffeine has declined substantially in fast metabolisers. A second dose on the bike, and a third at or before the run, is supported by pharmacokinetic modelling and is consistent with how professional triathletes structure caffeine intake.

**Evidence basis:** The ISSN 2021 Position Stand explicitly states: "multiple caffeine ingestion strategies may be used for extended exercise bouts." The distribution approach is therefore not experimental — it is a recognised application of caffeine pharmacokinetics to long-duration exercise.

### 3.5 Tolerance and Habitual Use

Habitual caffeine consumers develop tolerance at the receptor level (adenosine receptor upregulation). The performance-enhancing effect of a given dose is attenuated — not eliminated — in habitual users. The ISSN 2021 Position Stand recommends: habitual caffeine users should consume approximately 150mg above their habitual daily intake to achieve the same performance effect as a naive consumer at the standard 3–6 mg/kg dose.

**Caffeine withdrawal strategy:** Some athletes abstain from caffeine for 3–7 days prior to a race to restore receptor sensitivity. Evidence is mixed on whether this enhances the race-day effect (Irwin et al. 2011 suggests modest benefit; O'Callaghan et al. 2018 found no significant enhancement vs habitual use). It is an individual decision.

### 3.6 Safety Limits and Absolute Contraindications

**Upper safety threshold:** 9 mg/kg body weight produces adverse effects in most individuals — palpitations, GI distress, anxiety, insomnia. The ISSN 2021 Position Stand sets the practical upper limit at **400 mg/day** for healthy adults.

**Absolute contraindications:**
- **Caffeine + MAOI (monoamine oxidase inhibitor) interactions:** Potentially life-threatening. MAOIs prevent breakdown of catecholamines; caffeine-driven catecholamine release in the presence of MAO inhibition can cause hypertensive crisis, cardiac arrhythmia, and death. This is an absolute contraindication — no safe dose exists. Athletes on any MAOI class antidepressant (phenelzine, tranylcypromine, selegiline) must not receive caffeine-containing protocols.
- **Known cardiac arrhythmia:** Caffeine lowers the threshold for arrhythmia in susceptible individuals. Contraindicated per ISSN 2021 clinical guidance and consistent with cardiology practice guidelines for caffeine restriction in arrhythmia patients.
- **Severe anxiety disorder:** Caffeine exacerbates anxiety symptomatology via adenosine antagonism and catecholamine release. Not an absolute contraindication but requires dose reduction and explicit informed consent.

### 3.7 RIK Protocol Caffeine Budget

The RIK product stack delivers caffeine in two discrete products:

**Euphoria (pre-race):** 100 mg caffeine. Consumed 20–25 minutes before race start (T-25 to T-20). This timing accounts for the product's absorption profile reaching effective levels near gun time.

**GEL 100 CAF 100 (mid-run):** 100 mg caffeine. Consumed at approximately the halfway point of the run leg, when RPE and mental fatigue are typically at their daily peak.

**Total protocol caffeine: 200 mg.**

For a 70 kg athlete, this equals 2.86 mg/kg — within the lower end of the ISSN-recommended 3–6 mg/kg range. This is a deliberately conservative budget for the following reasons:
1. Positions below the threshold where GI side effects become meaningful
2. Avoids exceeding the tolerance threshold for most habitual caffeine consumers
3. Maintains a safe margin for athletes who consume caffeine-containing food or beverages independently (morning coffee, etc.)

For athletes >85 kg or with documented tolerance, operators may consider recommending an additional 100 mg mid-bike, bringing the budget to 300 mg (3.5 mg/kg for 85 kg = still within ISSN guidelines).

## 4. Gut Adaptation Science

### 4.1 Cox et al. (2010) — The Gut Training Intervention

*(Detailed in Section 1.4 above — cross-reference applies here.)*

The Cox et al. (2010) finding of 17% increased exogenous oxidation and reduced GI symptoms after 28 days of high-carbohydrate training sessions is the foundational evidence for the "gut training" concept. The molecular mechanism — SGLT1 upregulation — gives this finding biological credibility beyond just observed performance differences.

**Practical gut training protocol derived from this evidence:**
- Sessions ≥60 minutes in duration
- Target CHO intake: start at 45–60g/hr, increase by 10–15g/hr every 1–2 weeks toward race target
- Sessions must be at or near race intensity (absorption is reduced at low intensity)
- Duration: minimum 28 days before expecting adaptation; 8–12 weeks for reliable high-ceiling adaptation (80–90g/hr)

### 4.2 Pfeiffer et al. (2012) — GI History as Predictor

**Citation:** Pfeiffer B, Stellingwerff T, Hodgson AB, et al. Nutritional intake and gastrointestinal problems during competitive endurance events. *Med Sci Sports Exerc.* 2012;44(2):344–351.

**Design:** Survey of 221 triathletes and runners across multiple events. Collected retrospective GI symptom history and prospective race-day GI symptom data.

**Key finding:** Prior GI history was the **single strongest predictor of race-day GI failure.** Athletes who reported GI problems in previous races were significantly more likely to experience GI problems in the study race, independent of carbohydrate intake levels.

**Interpretation:** GI distress is not purely a dose-response phenomenon. It reflects underlying individual susceptibility — intestinal motility patterns, gut microbiome composition, gut permeability — that is stable within individuals. An athlete who has had GI problems before is not simply "taking too much" — they have a gut that is structurally more sensitive to exercise-induced perturbation. These athletes require lower carbohydrate ceilings, shorter gel dosing intervals, and more conservative product selection regardless of theoretical intake capacity.

**Protocol implication:** GI history is treated as a hard modifier in the RIK system, not a soft preference. GI history = significant → automatic -10g/hr from bracket minimum, flag for operator review, and conservative product recommendations.

### 4.3 Heat-Induced Gut Compromise

During high-intensity exercise in heat, splanchnic (gastrointestinal) blood flow is reduced by 40–80% compared to resting state. This is a consequence of sympathetic vasoconstriction diverting blood to working muscles and cutaneous vasodilation for thermoregulation.

The consequences of reduced gut blood flow:
1. **Slowed absorption:** All substrate absorption is compromised proportionally to blood flow reduction
2. **Increased intestinal permeability:** Hypoxic enterocytes exhibit disrupted tight junction function → "leaky gut" → bacterial lipopolysaccharide (LPS/endotoxin) translocates into systemic circulation
3. **Systemic inflammatory response:** Endotoxin triggers cytokine release → nausea, vomiting, cramping — symptoms indistinguishable from GI substrate overload
4. **Amplified symptom profile:** At the same carbohydrate intake level, athletes in heat experience significantly worse GI symptoms than in cool conditions

**Protocol implication:** Hot race conditions (>25°C) lower the effective carbohydrate ceiling independent of gut training status. In extreme heat (>32°C), the carbohydrate target should be at the lower bound of the athlete's bracket, not the upper bound.

*Sources: Otte JA et al. (2001). Exercise induces gastric ischemia in healthy volunteers. J Appl Physiol; Zuhl M et al. (2014). Exercise, intestinal permeability, and the influence of nutrition on gut integrity. Curr Sports Med Rep.*

### 4.4 Osmolality and Hydrogel Technology

Highly concentrated carbohydrate solutions create an osmotic gradient across the intestinal mucosa, drawing fluid into the lumen. Solutions above approximately 350 mOsm/kg are "hypertonic" and will impede water absorption (or draw water from tissues into the gut). This is the mechanism of osmotic diarrhea from concentrated gels consumed without sufficient water.

**Maurten hydrogel technology:** Sodium alginate and pectin form a gel at gastric pH (~2), which releases in the neutral pH of the small intestine. The gel matrix is proposed to physically sequester glucose and fructose, reducing effective luminal osmolality during transit through the upper GI tract. This allows higher carbohydrate concentrations to be ingested with lower osmotic stress on the intestinal mucosa.

*Source: Tsintzas K et al. (2020). Carbohydrate-rich supplementation and exercise. Nutrients. [Review including hydrogel evidence base]; Viribay A et al. (2020). Effects of 120 g/h of carbohydrates intake during a mountain marathon. Nutrients.*

### 4.5 Fructose Malabsorption

Fructose malabsorption (distinct from hereditary fructose intolerance) affects approximately 10–15% of the Western population. GLUT5 expression is reduced, and dietary fructose reaches the colon where fermentation by gut bacteria produces gas (hydrogen, methane), osmotic fluid shifts, and diarrhea — classic FODMAP symptomatology.

**Critical protocol implication:** Fructose malabsorption makes any MTC product a direct GI contraindication above 60g/hr. Above 60g/hr, fructose co-ingestion is required to exceed the SGLT1 ceiling — but in fructose malabsorbers, this fructose will not be absorbed and will instead cause diarrhea. The only evidence-based option for fructose malabsorbers needing >60g/hr is glucose polymer products (UCAN superstarch) at carefully titrated doses. In practice, the ceiling for fructose malabsorbers in the RIK protocol is **60g/hr maximum, hard cap**, regardless of bracket or gut training status.

*Source: Riby JE et al. (1993). Fructose absorption. Am J Clin Nutr; Gibson PR, Shepherd SJ (2010). Evidence-based dietary management of functional gastrointestinal symptoms: the FODMAP approach. J Gastroenterol Hepatol.*

## 5. RED-S and Female Athlete Considerations

### 5.1 IOC Consensus Statement 2018

**Citation:** Mountjoy M, Sundgot-Borgen JK, Burke LM, et al. IOC consensus statement on relative energy deficiency in sport (RED-S): 2018 update. *Br J Sports Med.* 2018;52(11):687–697.

Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S) is the syndromic consequence of insufficient energy availability (EA) to support both the metabolic requirements of the body and the energy cost of athletic training. RED-S is not equivalent to disordered eating, though it may coexist. An athlete can develop RED-S through inadequately fuelled training volume without psychological food restriction.

**Energy availability (EA) definition:**
EA = (Energy Intake − Exercise Energy Expenditure) ÷ Fat-Free Mass (kg LBM)

- Optimal EA: ≥45 kcal/kg LBM/day
- Subclinical RED-S: 30–45 kcal/kg LBM/day
- Clinically significant RED-S: <30 kcal/kg LBM/day

**Prevalence in female endurance athletes:** The IOC 2018 statement cites studies showing 30–50% of female endurance athletes exhibit markers of low energy availability. Triathlon-specific data is consistent with this range.

### 5.2 LEAF-Q Screening

**LEAF-Q (Low Energy Availability in Females Questionnaire):** A validated, self-report screening instrument for low energy availability in female athletes. Developed by Melin et al. (2014). Captures menstrual function, GI problems, and injury history — three domains that collectively predict clinical RED-S with high sensitivity and moderate specificity.

**Citation:** Melin A, Tornberg ÅB, Skouby S, et al. The LEAF questionnaire: a screening tool for the identification of female athletes at risk for the female athlete triad. *Br J Sports Med.* 2014;48(7):540–545.

LEAF-Q score ≥8 indicates high risk; ≤7 indicates lower risk. Screening does not diagnose RED-S — it triggers clinical referral.

### 5.3 Menstrual Disruption as Red Flag

Menstrual disruption — oligomenorrhea (cycle length >35 days) or amenorrhea (absence of menstruation for ≥3 consecutive months) — is the most clinically visible and functionally significant consequence of low energy availability. In a pre-menopausal female athlete who is not pregnant, menstrual disruption is RED-S until proven otherwise. Other causes (hypothyroidism, hyperprolactinaemia, PCOS) must be ruled out by clinical workup, but RED-S is the default diagnosis in endurance sport context.

**Protocol implication:** Menstrual disruption is flagged in the RIK intake system and triggers mandatory operator review before a high-output protocol is issued.

### 5.4 RED-S + High-Carbohydrate Protocol: The Paradox

A high-carbohydrate fuelling protocol (60–90g/hr during training sessions) does not correct underlying low energy availability. The interaction is paradoxical: an athlete consuming 80g of carbohydrate per hour during a 3-hour session is ingesting 240g carbohydrate (~960 kcal from CHO alone). This can give the appearance of adequate fuelling when daily total energy intake remains far below requirements.

**The diagnostic trap:** An athlete with RED-S may consume RIK products correctly during sessions and show appropriate training performance while their 24-hour energy balance remains critically deficient. The carbohydrate protocol does not cause harm in isolation — but it can mask the total energy deficit that is causing harm (hormonal suppression, bone demineralisation, immune compromise).

**Bone stress injury risk:** Athletes with RED-S have significantly reduced bone mineral density due to oestrogen suppression and IGF-1 disruption. In athletes with RED-S markers + high training volume, bone stress injury risk is elevated 3–4 fold compared to eumenorrheic athletes (Nattiv et al. 2007). High-intensity training protocols in RED-S athletes warrant additional caution.

**Fuelling targets for women:** Gram-per-hour carbohydrate targets are the same as for male athletes of equivalent bracket — the transporter biology and oxidation capacity are not sex-differentiated at equivalent training status. However, the total energy context matters more: a female athlete consuming 80g/hr during a 5-hour session must also consume adequate total energy in the surrounding 24 hours for the protocol to be safe and effective.

*Sources: Nattiv A, Loucks AB, Manore MM, et al. (2007). American College of Sports Medicine position stand: The female athlete triad. Med Sci Sports Exerc; De Souza MJ et al. (2014). 2014 Female Athlete Triad Coalition Consensus Statement. Br J Sports Med.*

## 6. Heat and Environmental Physiology

### 6.1 Core Temperature Thresholds

Core temperature regulation is central to exercise performance in heat. Relevant thresholds:

| Core temperature | Clinical status |
|-----------------|----------------|
| 37.0–37.5°C | Normal resting |
| 38.5–39.5°C | Typical during sustained endurance exercise |
| 39.0–39.5°C | Upper limit of sustainable exercise; performance begins to decline |
| 40.0°C | Heat illness threshold; exercise capacity severely compromised |
| >40.5°C | Exertional heat stroke — medical emergency |

Endurance athletes can sustain core temperatures of 39–39.5°C during competition without heat illness because of high skin blood flow and effective sweating. This is a trained adaptation, not a safety margin to be deliberately pushed.

*Source: Nybo L, Nielsen B. (2001). Hyperthermia and central fatigue during prolonged exercise in humans. J Appl Physiol.*

### 6.2 Sweat Rate in Heat

Sweat rate in exercising athletes: 0.5–2.5 L/hr (rest to heavy exercise in extreme heat). In Ironman triathlon conditions with air temperatures >30°C and high intensity:

- Bike leg: 1.0–2.0 L/hr (lower core temp and effective convective cooling at speed)
- Run leg: 1.5–2.5 L/hr (higher core temp, less convective cooling, upright posture)

These rates cannot be fully replaced during racing without exceeding GI absorption capacity for fluid (maximum ~1.0–1.2 L/hr from the gut). A net fluid deficit during racing is physiologically normal and does not require emergency correction. The target is to minimise the deficit, not eliminate it.

### 6.3 Pre-Cooling Strategies

**Ice vest:** Applied in the 10–30 minutes pre-race to reduce core temperature by 0.3–0.5°C before exercise begins. Lower starting core temperature delays the point at which temperature-dependent fatigue mechanisms activate. Effect on performance: 1–3% improvement in time-trial performance in heat (Tyler et al. 2015 meta-analysis).

**Cold towel / ice immersion:** Less effective than vest due to lower thermal mass contact. Still provides peripheral cooling and may reduce RPE.

**Menthol mouth rinse/gel:** Does not reduce core temperature but activates cold thermoreceptors in the oral cavity, reducing perceived thermal load. Performance benefit is real (approximately equivalent to modest core cooling) but mechanically different.

*Source: Tyler CJ, Reeve T, Hodges GJ, Cheung SS. (2015). The effects of heat adaptation on physiology, perception and exercise performance in the heat: a meta-analysis. Sports Med.*

### 6.4 Heat Acclimatisation

Physiological adaptations to heat occur with 8–14 days of daily heat exposure (exercise in hot conditions, or passive heat exposure such as sauna):

1. **Plasma volume expansion:** 4–12% increase in plasma volume reduces cardiovascular strain
2. **Earlier onset of sweating:** Core temperature threshold for sweat initiation decreases (~0.2–0.3°C lower)
3. **Higher sweat rate at lower core temperature:** More efficient thermoregulatory response
4. **Lower sweat sodium concentration:** Aldosterone-driven renal and sweat gland sodium conservation

**Athletes racing in heat without prior acclimatisation** have significantly higher risk of heat illness and will experience greater performance decrement than acclimatised athletes. For races in heat (>28°C), 10–14 days of structured acclimatisation is standard recommendation from ACSM and IOC guidance.

### 6.5 Heat Effects on Carbohydrate Oxidation

At elevated core temperatures, glucose oxidation efficiency is reduced. The mechanism involves direct temperature effects on enzyme kinetics and increased sympathetic activation that diverts metabolic priority toward heat dissipation. Combined with the gut blood flow reduction (Section 4.3), this creates a double constraint: athletes need carbohydrate more as RPE increases, but can absorb less of it.

**Protocol implication:** In extreme heat (>32°C), **do not increase carbohydrate intake above the bracket midpoint**. The gut cannot process high carbohydrate loads efficiently at high core temperatures. Maintain or reduce carbohydrate intake; increase electrolyte delivery; ensure adequate fluid availability. If GI symptoms emerge, reduce carbohydrate rate immediately rather than continuing to push intake.

## 7. Ironman Triathlon Race Physiology

### 7.1 Energy Expenditure

Full Ironman energy expenditure estimates based on metabolic equivalents (METs) and measured rates:

- Total race energy expenditure: **7,000–10,000 kcal** (body weight, speed, efficiency dependent)
- Swim (3.8 km): approximately 700–900 kcal (~50–80 min)
- Bike (180 km): approximately 4,000–6,000 kcal (~4.5–8.5 hr)
- Run (42.2 km): approximately 2,800–3,500 kcal (~3–6 hr)

**Endogenous carbohydrate stores:**
- Muscle glycogen (~400g): ~1,600 kcal
- Liver glycogen (~100g): ~400 kcal
- Total: ~2,000 kcal of available carbohydrate

**Implication:** Even consuming 600g of exogenous carbohydrate (the upper bound of what most age-group athletes can absorb), total carbohydrate available is ~4,400 kcal — less than half the race energy expenditure. The remainder is provided by fat oxidation. The goal of carbohydrate fuelling is not to replace all energy, but to supplement the carbohydrate pathway at intensities where fat cannot supply fuel fast enough.

### 7.2 Glycogen Depletion Kinetics

At moderate-to-high race intensity (70–80% VO₂max):
- Muscle glycogen depletion onset: symptomatic fatigue begins at approximately **1.5–2 hours** of continuous exercise without exogenous carbohydrate
- Complete endogenous depletion: approximately **2.5–3 hours** (intensity-dependent)
- "Bonking" or "hitting the wall": the subjective experience of glycogen depletion — sudden profound fatigue, cognitive impairment, loss of pace

**First gel timing derives directly from this:** If depletion begins at 1.5–2 hours and the first gel is consumed at 45 minutes on the bike (having had a pre-race gel at T-25), the athlete has maintained a supply chain to the muscle with no gap. If the first gel is delayed until "when I feel tired," depletion has already begun and performance is compromised before any corrective action can take effect.

### 7.3 The Swim Leg: Fuelling Considerations

GI stress during swimming is minimal — the horizontal position, lack of vertical impact, and low relative intensity (most athletes swim at moderate effort to preserve energy) create the most favourable GI conditions of the race. Fluid intake during the swim is impractical (salt water ingestion is unavoidable for open-water swimmers).

**Opportunity:** The swim represents a gut-recovery window after pre-race nerves may have compressed gut blood flow. Athletes who consumed pre-race food within 2 hours may benefit from the swim allowing partial gastric emptying before the bike fuelling window begins.

**Gel in the water:** Some athletes with dedicated aero helmets have experimented with taking a gel at swim exit. Not recommended in standard protocols — execution complexity is high, and the gain over starting bike fuelling within the first 10–15 minutes is marginal.

### 7.4 The Bike Leg: Primary Fuelling Window

The bike leg is the physiologically optimal fuelling window in triathlon for the following reasons:
1. **Horizontal, seated position:** Reduces intra-abdominal pressure and gut motility compared to upright running
2. **Lower mechanical impact:** No vertical ground reaction forces jarring the GI tract
3. **Better splanchnic blood flow:** At equivalent relative intensity, cycling produces better GI perfusion than running
4. **Practical access:** Aero bottles, bento boxes, and jersey pockets enable consistent access to nutrition

**Protocol priority:** The majority of race-day carbohydrate intake should occur on the bike. Athletes who defer carbohydrate intake to the run are operating against the physiology and will experience GI problems at lower intake levels than they would on the bike.

**Carbohydrate targets by bracket: see Section 8.**

### 7.5 T2 and the Run Leg: Managing the Decline

**T2 (Bike-to-Run Transition):** The final opportunity to consume nutrition before run-GI stress becomes a dominant factor. A gel immediately before dismounting the bike or at the start of T2 provides substrate available at approximately the 20–30 minute mark of the run — when the athlete is settling into run pace and GI stress is still manageable. This is a strategic window that is frequently missed by athletes.

**Run leg physiology:**
- Vertical ground reaction forces: 2–3× body weight per stride → direct mechanical stimulation of gut
- Increased gut transit speed from impact
- Continued splanchnic blood flow reduction (typically more intense run pace relative to swim/bike for age-group athletes)
- Cortisol and catecholamine levels at their race-day peak

**Practical consequence:** GI tolerance on the run is 30–40% lower than on the bike for the same product type. An athlete who tolerated 80g/hr on the bike should not attempt more than 50–55g/hr on the run. Gels should be chased with more water on the run than on the bike. Concentrated solutions are worse on the run than during any other leg.

### 7.6 Two Failure Modes: Bonking vs GI Failure

**Bonk/glycogen depletion:** Caused by insufficient carbohydrate intake relative to demand. Presents as: profound fatigue, slowing pace, difficulty maintaining concentration, weakness in legs but no GI symptoms. The correction is carbohydrate — but if the athlete has already bonked, cognitive impairment makes self-correction difficult. Prevention is the only effective strategy.

**GI failure:** Caused by excessive or inappropriate carbohydrate (or caffeine) intake relative to gut absorption capacity. Presents as: nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, bloating, cramping. The correction is to stop ingesting carbohydrates and allow the gut to clear — which further deepens glycogen depletion. GI failure cascades into bonking.

**The protocol design challenge:** Both failure modes cause DNF. The optimal protocol threads between them — enough carbohydrate to prevent glycogen depletion, not so much (or of the wrong type) as to cause GI failure. This is the central engineering problem the RIK protocol tool is designed to solve.

## 8. Evidence-Based Carbohydrate Targets by Athlete Bracket

### 8.1 Bracket Classification

Athletes are classified by finish time as the best available proxy for metabolic efficiency, exercise intensity, gut adaptation level, and total race duration — all of which modulate optimal carbohydrate intake.

| Bracket | Full IM finish time | 70.3 finish time | Notes |
|---------|--------------------|--------------------|-------|
| Rookie | >13 hr | >6.5 hr | First or second race; gut adaptation unlikely |
| Seasoned | 11–13 hr | 5.5–6.5 hr | Multiple race experience; partial gut training |
| Experienced | 10–11 hr | 5.0–5.5 hr | Competitive age-grouper; likely gut-trained |
| Sub-10/Elite AG | <10 hr | <5.0 hr | Highly trained; full gut adaptation expected |

### 8.2 Carbohydrate Targets by Bracket

| Bracket | Full IM — Bike g/hr | Full IM — Run g/hr | 70.3 — Bike g/hr | 70.3 — Run g/hr |
|---------|--------------------|--------------------|------------------|-----------------|
| Rookie | 45–60 | 30–40 | 45–60 | 30–40 |
| Seasoned | 60–80 | 40–55 | 60–80 | 40–55 |
| Experienced | 75–90 | 50–65 | 75–90 | 50–65 |
| Sub-10/Elite AG | 90–110 | 60–75 | 90–110 | 60–75 |

**Run target derivation:** Run targets are set at approximately **bike × 0.65** (midpoint of the evidence-based 30–40% reduction). Always round DOWN to the nearest 5g/hr to avoid the GI risk zone.

*Sources: Jeukendrup AE (2011). Nutrition for endurance sports: marathon, triathlon, and road cycling. J Sports Sci; Burke LM et al. (2016) — ACSM Joint Position Stand.*

### 8.3 Modifiers (Applied in Stacking Order)

The following modifiers apply to the bracket baseline and stack in the order listed. Each modifier is applied to the current value after the previous modifier has been applied.

1. **GI history = significant (prior race GI events):** −10g/hr from bracket minimum
   *Basis: Pfeiffer et al. (2012) — prior GI history is the strongest predictor of race-day GI failure.*

2. **Gut training status:**
   - None (no deliberate gut training): −10g/hr
   - Partial (some high-carb sessions but below race targets): −5g/hr
   - Trained (consistent training at race-level carb intake for ≥28 days): 0g/hr
   *Basis: Cox et al. (2010) — gut adaptation requires minimum 28 days of exposure.*

3. **maxComfortableCarbsPerHour provided by athlete:** If the athlete's self-reported comfortable maximum is lower than the bracket value after modifiers 1–2, cap at that value. This is a hard override — never prescribe above athlete-stated comfort ceiling.

4. **Fructose malabsorption confirmed:** Hard cap at 60g/hr regardless of bracket or modifiers. No MTC products permitted.
   *Basis: Section 4.5 above.*

5. **Hot race conditions (>25°C):** Apply lower bound of post-modifier range, not upper bound.
   *Basis: Section 4.3 — gut blood flow reduction in heat.*

## 9. Fuelling Windows and Absorption Timing

### 9.1 Carbohydrate Absorption Kinetics

Carbohydrate absorption from the gut to systemic circulation follows predictable but product-dependent kinetics:

| Product format | Time to measurable blood glucose rise | Time to peak |
|---------------|--------------------------------------|-------------|
| Liquid (dilute drink mix) | 10–15 min | 20–30 min |
| Gel (standard) | 15–20 min | 25–35 min |
| Gel (with water co-ingestion) | 15–20 min | 25–35 min |
| Solid food (bar, banana) | 20–30 min | 40–60 min |
| Maurten hydrogel | 15–20 min (similar to gel) | 25–35 min |

**Practical implication:** The common athlete practice of consuming a gel "when I start to feel tired" delivers glucose to the bloodstream 15–20 minutes after the feeling of fatigue — by which time the athlete has been performing in a carbohydrate-compromised state for 30+ minutes. Proactive timing is mandatory.

### 9.2 Gel Dosing Interval

**Evidence-based interval: every 20–30 minutes.**

Many athlete instructions and packaging suggest 45–60-minute intervals. This is incorrect for racing purposes. The goal is continuous substrate delivery — maintaining blood glucose in the performance range (4.5–7 mmol/L during exercise) without peaks and troughs. A 45-minute interval between gels creates a trough that is likely to cause subjective fatigue and may be detectable in measured power output.

At a 25-minute interval: a 60g/hr target requires one 25g gel every 25 minutes (i.e., two gels per hour). A 90g/hr target requires either a 45g gel every 30 minutes or three 30g gels per hour.

*Sources: Wallis GA et al. (2007). Oxidation of combined ingestion of maltodextrins and fructose during exercise. Med Sci Sports Exerc; practical application from Jeukendrup 2011 review.*

### 9.3 First Gel Timing: The Early-Start Rule

**Rule: First gel within the first 20 minutes of the bike leg.**

Rationale: Glycogen depletion begins from the moment of race start. The swim depletes muscle glycogen at low-to-moderate rate (~30–50g depending on duration and intensity). By the time T1 is complete and the athlete is on the bike, 45–80 minutes of racing may have elapsed. If the first gel is not consumed until 45 minutes into the bike, glycogen has been depleting for 90–120 minutes with no exogenous supplementation. This is the pathway to bonking in the back half of the bike or early run.

**Pre-race gel at T-25:** Consumed 25 minutes before race start (alongside Euphoria). This gel's glucose reaches systemic circulation at approximately gun time. It provides substrate for the swim and bridges to the first bike gel.

### 9.4 Electrolyte Timing: Buccal vs GI Onset

**FastChews (buccal delivery):** Begin electrolyte delivery within 2–5 minutes of oral dissolution. Should be started **at the race start gun**, not delayed until symptoms emerge. Symptom-reactive electrolyte supplementation is always too late — by the time cramping or low-sodium symptoms appear, the plasma sodium deficit has been accumulating for 30–60 minutes.

**FastChews dosing interval during racing:** Every 30 minutes in temperate conditions; every 20 minutes in heat. Set a repeating timer — do not rely on symptoms as the trigger.

**Swallowed electrolyte products (capsules, tablets):** 30–60 minutes to peak plasma sodium elevation. Must be administered earlier than buccal products to achieve the same timeline.

### 9.5 Caffeine Timing

**Pre-race Euphoria at T-25:** Accounts for the 45-minute time-to-peak plasma concentration — Euphoria is absorbed faster than traditional caffeine sources (formulation design), estimated 30–40 minutes to peak. Starting at T-25 delivers peak adenosine receptor occupancy at approximately gun time.

**Mid-run caffeine (GEL 100 CAF 100):** Consumed at approximately the halfway point of the run. For a 4-hour marathon leg, this is at the 2-hour mark. For a 3-hour run, at the 1.5-hour mark. This provides the second wave of adenosine antagonism during the most psychologically demanding portion of the race.

### 9.6 Solid Food: Bike Only, Early Window

**Evidence-based rule:** Solid food (bars, real food, bananas) should be consumed during the **first 60–90 minutes of the bike only.** After this window:

1. Splanchnic blood flow has declined sufficiently that solid food digestion is impaired
2. Core temperature has risen, further compromising gut function
3. Exercise intensity may have increased as athletes settle into race effort
4. Remaining race time is better served by precisely dosed liquid and gel carbohydrates

**Solid food on the run: never.** No evidence supports solid food consumption during the run leg of an Ironman. The mechanical impact, gut blood flow, and time-to-absorption all make solid food the worst possible choice on the run. Any protocol instruction to consume solid food during the run is inconsistent with the evidence base.

*Source: Rehrer NJ et al. (1992). Effect of exercise on gut function and gut hormones. Eur J Appl Physiol.*

## 12. Hard Lines That Cannot Be Crossed (Safety Boundaries)

These constraints represent absolute protocol limits. No bracket, no modifier, no operator override, and no athlete preference can push a protocol past these boundaries. Each is traceable to primary evidence or established clinical safety guidance.

---

**1. Never exceed 120g/hr carbohydrate — physiological absorption ceiling**

The maximum exogenous carbohydrate oxidation rate documented in the literature under optimal conditions (elite athletes, full gut adaptation, 2:1 MTC products) is approximately 1.75g/min (~105g/hr). The absolute ceiling is 120g/hr; above this, intestinal transit capacity becomes rate-limiting and osmotic diarrhea becomes near-universal. No evidence supports exceeding 120g/hr as beneficial.

*Source: Jeukendrup AE (2010). Curr Sports Med Rep.*

---

**2. Never exceed 400 mg caffeine/day — ISSN safety limit**

The ISSN 2021 Position Stand sets 400 mg/day as the practical upper limit for healthy adults. Doses above this threshold increase adverse event frequency (palpitations, GI distress, anxiety, tremor) without proportional performance benefit. The RIK 200 mg budget provides a 200 mg safety margin.

*Source: Guest et al. (2021). J Int Soc Sports Nutr.*

---

**3. Any product delivering >60g/hr must be MTC-based — no exceptions**

Above 60g/hr, the SGLT1 transporter is saturated. Any carbohydrate above this rate that is not fructose (using GLUT5) will not be absorbed, will accumulate in the intestinal lumen, and will cause osmotic diarrhea. Prescribing a glucose-only or maltodextrin-only product at targets above 60g/hr is a direct protocol error with predictable GI failure consequences.

*Source: Jeukendrup & Jentjens (2000); Jentjens et al. (2004).*

---

**4. Never instruct athlete to "drink as much as possible" — thirst is the guide**

Overdrinking of plain or low-sodium fluid is the primary cause of exercise-associated hyponatremia — a potentially fatal condition. The correct hydration instruction is: **drink to thirst.** Athletes should not be instructed to consume a fixed high volume of fluid, particularly during running when the urge to drink may be elevated by heat stress while actual sweat losses may not warrant the volume consumed.

*Source: Noakes TD (2012). Waterlogged; Speedy et al. (1999). Med Sci Sports Exerc.*

---

**5. Fructose malabsorption: hard cap at 60g/hr, no MTC products permitted**

Athletes with confirmed fructose malabsorption cannot use any product that contains fructose as a co-transport substrate. This eliminates all MTC products (which require fructose to exceed 60g/hr). The hard cap is 60g/hr of glucose or glucose polymer products. This is not a preference — it is a contraindication based on transporter biology.

*Source: Gibson & Shepherd (2010). J Gastroenterol Hepatol; Section 4.5 above.*

---

**6. Solid food: early bike only — never during the run**

No evidence supports solid food consumption during the run leg of an Ironman triathlon. The combination of impact-induced gut motility, reduced splanchnic blood flow, and the slow absorption kinetics of solid food makes the run leg incompatible with solid food fuelling. Any protocol prescribing solid food on the run is not evidence-based. The window for solid food is the **first 60–90 minutes of the bike leg only.**

*Source: Rehrer et al. (1990, 1992); Section 9.6 above.*

---

**7. MAOI + caffeine: absolute contraindication — no caffeine in protocol**

If an athlete is taking any monoamine oxidase inhibitor (MAOI class antidepressant or other MAOI medication), caffeine is absolutely contraindicated. This overrides all bracket recommendations and operator discretion. The interaction can cause hypertensive crisis and life-threatening cardiac events. The protocol tool must flag MAOI use and remove all caffeine-containing products.

*Source: ISSN 2021 Position Stand (Guest et al. 2021); clinical pharmacology of MAOI-sympathomimetic interactions.*

---

**8. Cardiac arrhythmia: caffeine contraindicated**

Athletes with diagnosed cardiac arrhythmia (AF, SVT, WPW, or other arrhythmia on antiarrhythmic medication) should not receive caffeine-containing protocol recommendations without explicit cardiology clearance. This applies to Euphoria and all caffeinated gel recommendations.

*Source: ISSN 2021 Position Stand; cardiology practice consensus.*
